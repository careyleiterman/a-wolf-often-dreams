Scared of a Cockroach

March 7th, 2020

I was six years old, living in Las Vegas, Nevada. My parents had just gotten divorced and each of them decided to move back to New York City, New York. My dad moved to Canarsie, at the time a mostly middle class neighborhood, while my mother moved to an area near Coney Island Avenue, a mix of mostly low to medium-low income housing. The courts had decided that my sister and I were going to be better off in the custody of my mother, even though I remember wanting to live with my dad, so that is where we ended up. It seems the courts don‘t take the word of a child very seriously, and somehow assume that in a single parent home, mothers are going to be better at parenting than the father might be. In the case of my mother, however, this was not the case. Although my life was influenced to a great degree by her lack of ‘mothering‘, the thing that I remember the most, and that has probably caused me the most problems in my life wasn‘t her bad parenting at all.

1402, avenue k, Brooklyn, New York. That is where my building was; it was in a bad neighborhood, on a bad street. It was one in a line of about six or seven buildings, each taking up a full city block. Each building was six stories tall, and each one more dilapidated than the other. I lived in perhaps the worst of them. Well at least I had assumed it was the worst of them. I was never actually inside the other buildings and could only go by what I had seen from the outside. But I was six and well, my building just had to be the worst.

Overall, the building might have looked good fifty years ago, but now the only term I can use to describe the building was slum. The old red bricks, once brilliant and clean, were now dingy and starting to crumble. Many of the windows were broken; some were even covered with boards instead of having been repaired. The main entrance was also disgusting, filled with trash and other things that you shouldn‘t see outside a building. There was so much feces at the entrance that I became paranoid about stepping in it, forcing me to walk with my head down instead of looking where I was going.

The inside was no better; it was as neglected as the outside, so as you might imagine, since the outside was so horrible I was miserable. And the worst part of it all? I was forced to stay indoors almost constantly for the next five years. The next five years. Probably the worst five years of my life.

Now obviously, as you can probably tell, I experienced no joy where I lived. After all, what child would want to live in a slum-like tenement? But even with all the filth and dilapidation, around me during my childhood, one thing stood out more than the rest. As I said earlier, the building was old, practically a slum. It also happened to be the perfect breeding ground for roaches. Now I know that most of you probably ‘love‘ the little critters; the way they crawl up and down the walls, how they fill your kitchen sinks and dishes, even the way they hide among your forks and knives in a drawer. But me on the other hand, I have to admit that I have no love for them. In a world ruled by human beings, my life was ruled by roaches.

The first time I saw one of the little brown beasts I didn‘t know what they were. It was about half an inch long with a dull brown body. Its tiny eyes were attached to a rather disgusting looking head, even for a bug, which ended in two long antenna. Even its legs were gross. Hairy and long they looked somewhat like those of a cricket but at the same time different. When I asked my mother what it was, she told me it was a roach and then proceeded to kill it with either bug spray or a shoe. Or maybe it was a paper towel or newspaper. The truth is, there were numerous ways to kill the little guys and you just used what was close by at the time. After she killed it, I didn‘t really think much of it, and at first, I only found them to be a minor annoyance. But after a while, I began to notice more of them. At first it was only a few, but then it was a dozen, then more. A realization came with their numbers. I lived in their apartment. They did not live in mine. It was bad enough that three people (my mother, my sister and I) had to share the small one bedroom, but it was worse to find out that we were not the only tenants.

One of my favorite things to do was to wake up early on a Saturday to watch the early morning cartoons. I‘d sit on the floor after making a bowl of cereal, turn on the volume really low, and watch the Superfriends or whatever else might be on at six or seven am. Unfortunately, unlike other children who did the same thing with their siblings, I had other company. It didn‘t really take long before my friends would come out to watch with me. It might be only one or two of the hairy-winged bastards, but it was usually more. They brought their friends. They brought their families. Even their distant relatives seemed to come to join the fun. And oh how fun that was! Little ones the size of a letter on this page, big ones, almost an inch long, with their large antennae flicking back and forth along the ground; all of them came for the fun.

Unfortunately, for them anyway, most of them died a quick death under a nearby shoe. (I always kept a shoe close by when I sat on the floor to watch television) Although at first this was only a somewhat annoying, as time progressed, it got worse. Each day seemed to bring out more roaches. Each week more of them made themselves at home. The apartment, as time went on, became infested with them. And with this infestation, my problem started.

I am not sure of the exact day or week that it really began to bother me, but eventually rather than being merely annoyed by them, I began to fear them. Not a fear like that of being eaten alive by a shark, or even the fear of heights many people have. No. This fear was more of a fear of the unexpected, kind of like the fear people have of spiders, but much, much worse. I started to dread killing them. The feel of their bodies being smashed in a paper towel, the sound of them popping as their bodies burst apart and making popping sounds made me cringe. Seeing half of a body, ripped apart on the ground, or on the wall would make me gag to the point where I might vomit. Each roach brought more death, more hideous sounds and more torn apart bodies. Each death increased in me, a fear that I could not understand. They were just roaches after all. Or were they?

Apparently, for me at least they were not just roaches. For me, they were a nightmare. They purposely walked around the apartment just to find me. They would torment me. If I wanted to eat, I would find them on my spoon when I pulled it from a drawer. They were in my cereal, in my bowl. When I went to sleep, I could see them on the walls, staring down into my eyes, waiting for me to close them so that they could attack me. Eventually I had to resort to making a cocoon out of my blankets so that they couldn‘t get to me. I would wrap my blanket so tightly around my body that I would wake in the middle of the night soaking wet from the heat. But this wasn’t even the worst of it. No. The worst of it was that time in the night that I would wake up and have to use the bathroom. Sure, they were in the bedroom and the kitchen, in the living room and in the hall. But the one place I dreaded to go, the one place they lurked most often in the dark, the one place I knew I could not escape them, was the bathroom. This is where I knew they waited. Everyone was asleep. They would torment me well.

Dark as pitch, I would walk ever so slowly on my tiptoes in hopes that with less of my feet on the ground, I might avoid stepping on them. Sometimes I made it. Most of the time I didn‘t. When I didn‘t, what I would feel can only be described as dread. The body of a roach, smashed against or even between my toes. I couldn‘t stand it. I would freak out. I would try to get to the bathroom as fast as possible to turn on the light. I would step on more, causing me to panic and shake. The light would save me. They hated the light. They would run from it. The light would save me. But as the blessed light went on, I would see it, their army, hundreds of them. Scattering like blown sand, they would be everywhere. Into the cracks they would run. Up the walls. Towards me. I panicked even more. Jumping and hopping I would lean against the wall in order to avoid them. I probably looked like some kind of contortionist, the way I was bending and moving. For good reason. I was terrified. If too many came my way, I would jump over them, hoping to land on a clean spot on the floor. More often than not, I just landed on more roaches. More often than not, my fears just made the situation worse. I would kill more than I avoided. In my panic I had no control.

Then, after what seemed like forever, I would finally make my way inside the bathroom. I had to check the sink. I checked the walls. I even looked up at the ceiling. I had to check the toilet basin to make sure there were none hiding in the bowl. I looked around it. Under it. Inside it. It was torture.

And every night it was the same ordeal. Use the bathroom or avoid the roaches. It was a battle I had to fight every night for five years. I became paranoid. Obsessive. I became a different person because of a bug. Because of an insect, I couldn‘t enjoy my life. Eventually I couldn‘t even use the bathroom anymore. I would pee on the radiator in the bedroom to avoid them. It would stink when the heat was on. But I didn‘t care. I didn‘t want to be near them. The strangest thing? My mother never asked me why the radiator smelled like pee. She had to have noticed the smell didn‘t she? What normal person wouldn’‘t? But perhaps that was part of my problem. My mother wasn’t normal.

Some five years after my ordeal began, when I was about eleven years old, my father seized custody of my sister and I, sparing me any more torture. Little did I know however, the effect those years would have on the rest of my life. I have since become extremely uncomfortable around any insects that crawl around. Whether spiders or beetles, roaches or ants, the discomfort I feel when I come across one unexpectantly is unpleasant to say the least. To this day I cannot step on anything that looks like a roach without it bringing back horrible memories. And when I do by accident, I cringe just as I did as a child. I remember living in Los Angeles when I was around twenty seven. I was walking down the street one evening and nearly stepped on a roach. From that point on I had to look down as I walked, though I tried to avoid the darker, unlit areas where they might be. To my aunt and uncle I looked ridiculous. They even made fun of me a little when they saw how I would avoid stepping on them. But to me? To me it was different. It took me back to the time when I was living in that apartment and I feel like I did when I was a child.

I am convinced that this is what started me on the road to developing obsessive compulsive disorder. I know it is a phobia. I know the reason I have it. I have even tried to cure it by facing my fear. But nothing I do works. So I take each day as it comes and I try not to worry about that aspect of my life so much.

But to this day, if I feel an itching on my leg when I am laying in bed, I can‘t help but wonder if it might be a roach. Then I ask myself. Are all my fears and anxiety worth it? Should I really be, ‘Scared of a Cockroach‘?

Corona Virus and Me

March 15th, 2020

So here are are. It is Sunday, March 15th and we as a city and a nation for that matter are panicking. I am not saying that we should not be cautious, we should. However, as someone who is prone to bouts of anxiety and panic attacks, I for one am in the opinion that how we are reacting is not good for the greater good.

I went to the local Target last night and there was nothing on any of the shelves in the medical aisles. Shelves that once held tissues, cough medicines, alcohol, and sanitizer were wiped clean. I almost laughed as I saw the shelves because I didn't know what to do.

My dad, who is 76 years old needed hydrogen peroxide because he constantly cuts himself due to thin skin and veins close to the surface. Because of people hording supplies, my dad, who actually needs it, has to go without an important item in his medicine chest. This, all because people are buying more than they actually need instead of just being cautious.

Even my stepmother, who is 80 years old, is overacting. She actually called me to tell me to buy alcohol from the liquor store to use as a cleaner. I truly couldn't believe what I was hearing. In her panic to keep the house clean, she was willing to use a product (a very expensive one at that) to clean the home. The only problem? Store bought alcohol, for the most part does not have enough alcohol to kill the virus. Except for maybe everyclear which is over 90% alcohol, most store bought alcohols have less than 50% alcohol content. For context. A beer has less than 10% alcohol in it.

She isn't the only one who obviously is in a panic. Many of my coworkers have become nervous and are starting to debate going in to work. This is a problem since I work for a school and if teachers do not show up, then the entire plan for kids to go to school is moot. Something has to give.

Even as I sit here, in a Starbucks coffee shop, I see people coming in, buying their coffee, most of whom are talking about it. They still come in to get their drinks, but most leave quickly, not sitting down at the tables now spaced 6' apart from each other. And yet, here I am. I am not panicked. I am concerned. This is a bit of an interesting turn of events since I am normally one to have high anxiety levels for less important things.

Suddenly, a man starts hacking up coughing about 4 feet from me. It is so loud and disturbing that I turn to him and look. He continues to cough and it begins to make me nervous. I debate leaving, as my anxiety levels begin to rise and I worry that possibly he has the virus. I feel the urge to leave and go home.

So what do I do? I fight it. I am going to finish this post and not be scared to live my life. I know in my mind that the virus will at most get me very sick, and at least show few symptoms. As someone with high anxiety I will myself to live my life outside of fear. I will stay.

So, for today. I have won my battle with anxiousness and panic. I fought with my OCD to allow me to live my life as I normally do. I urge you all, when you begin to worry, as you will, to think about the effects that worry will have on your life. If we worry constantly, it will only affect us in a negative way. If we live our lives, and take things as they come, we might run into problems, but we will always be able to find a solution.

With that, I leave you for today. Be well and remember. You are your own worst enemy, but you can also be your own best friend. Until next time. Carry on!
